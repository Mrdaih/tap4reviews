Asking for reviews feels cheap. Begging feels worse. And yet every successful restaurant we've worked with collects reviews at scale. They don't do it by being pushier — they do it by engineering four quiet moments into the guest journey.

## 1. Only ask when the meal is clearly great

Don't ask on autopilot. Train your staff to read one signal: has the guest said something positive during or after the meal? That's your green light. If the table was quiet or the feedback was mixed, skip the ask. You'll save yourself the occasional 2-star review and keep your average high.

The highest-performing restaurants ask maybe 40–50% of tables, not 100%.

## 2. Make it a single-sentence script

Forget the long explanation. The only sentence that works is: **"Loved it? Tap here."**

That's it. No preamble, no guilt, no promise of a discount (which is against Google's terms and will get your listing flagged). Hand over a Tap4Reviews card, smile, and walk away.

## 3. Time it with the bill

Psychologically, the single best moment is when the guest is already reaching for their phone to settle the bill. Their device is unlocked, in their hand, and their mindset is transactional — perfect for a 10-second detour into your Google review page.

Place the card inside the bill folder. Don't make a ceremony of it.

## 4. Close the loop

When reviews come in, respond to them. Good ones get a short, warm thank-you. Bad ones get a public, professional response — never defensive, always inviting the guest back.

Google's algorithm rewards engaged pages. Replies also signal to prospects reading reviews that someone is home.

## What NOT to do

- **Don't offer discounts or free items for reviews.** This is the fastest way to get your listing suspended.
- **Don't cherry-pick only happy customers to ask.** Ask everyone who had a genuinely great experience — full stop.
- **Don't send follow-up emails asking for reviews.** UAE guests rarely act on them; it feels spammy and conversion is under 0.5%.
- **Don't have staff write reviews on your behalf.** Google detects patterns and your entire listing can be penalised.

## The compounding effect

The first 100 reviews are the hardest. Past that, social proof starts doing the heavy lifting — guests see the volume and assume the restaurant is a safe bet. At 500+ reviews, your Google listing becomes a silent, 24/7 salesperson working alongside your actual team.

The shortest path from here to there is removing friction. Stop asking awkwardly. Let the card do the ask, and let the tap do the work.
